Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) is intended to function as a continuous blood filtration system for patients with kidney failure, especially when the patient is too unstable for regular dialysis (Nationwide Children’s Hospital, n.d.). It helps control fluid levels, manage toxins, and support kidney function over time in critically ill patients. CRRT’s performance is often limited by an incomplete understanding of how fluid and solute transport work during operation (Nandhabalan et al., 2021). This problem shows up during treatment in intensive care settings where precise control of fluid removal is necessary. The issue occurs because CRRT depends on several membrane transport processes such as pressure-driven flow, diffusion, convection, and particle adsorption (Nandhabalan et al., 2021). Operators must set treatment goals without clear or unified guidance on how these processes interact in real time. As a result, the system may remove fluids or solutes at rates that are inconsistent or not ideal. The gap exists between the current situation, where CRRT is used with limited predictive insight into its transport behavior, and the desired situation, where engineers and clinicians can accurately model, control, and optimize the filtration process to achieve steady and targeted removal of fluid and waste.

<br>

Black, Hispanic, and lower-income patients experiencing more severe kidney injury receive advanced treatments later or less often (Sutherland et al., 2019). Even when CRRT is provided, hospitals with fewer resources often deliver less consistent therapy due to staffing shortages or frequent machine issues. After hospitalization, patients from disadvantaged backgrounds may also struggle to access follow-up kidney care, increasing their risk of long-term complications (Sutherland et al., 2019). Health inequities in advanced kidney therapies, including CRRT, are closely tied to broader structural and social determinants of health. Black Americans are three times more likely than white Americans to develop end-stage renal disease despite representing only about 13 percent of the U.S. population, and the long-term use of race-adjusted eGFR equations historically delayed referrals and transplant eligibility for many Black patients. These systemic issues extend into acute care, where CRRT availability depends heavily on hospital resources; low-income and rural hospitals often lack the specialized machines, staffing levels, and nephrology support required to deliver consistent therapy. Financial barriers further limit access, as patients without strong insurance coverage are more likely to experience delayed initiation or shorter treatment duration. Racial and socioeconomic patterns compound these gaps, with marginalized patients experiencing higher rates of acute kidney injury but receiving advanced therapies less consistently or at later stages (Sutherland et al., 2019). Even after hospitalization, disadvantaged patients often face obstacles such as limited nephrology follow-up, transportation challenges, and fragmented care, increasing long-term risk. These disparities highlight that improving CRRT outcomes requires not only advancing technical understanding of the therapy but also addressing inequities that shape who receives timely and effective treatment.
