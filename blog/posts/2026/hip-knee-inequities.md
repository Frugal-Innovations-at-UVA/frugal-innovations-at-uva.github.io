![Hip morphology differences diagram](/assets/blog/2026/hip-knee-inequities.jpg)

Hip and knee replacements are among the most common implants worldwide, with roughly 540,000 hip replacements performed annually in the U.S.1 Despite this, devices have largely been designed around male anatomy, leading to poorer outcomes for women. For example, women have a 29% higher risk of hip implant failure.

<br>

Women’s anatomy and physiology differ from men: they typically have smaller femoral dimensions, different pelvic geometry, greater ligament laxity, lower tendon stiffness, and higher rates of metal sensitivity.2 Standardized implants often fail to account for these differences, causing leg length discrepancies, altered gait, and increased mechanical stress. Population studies also show that women are less likely to receive hip or knee replacements even when clinically indicated.3

<br>

Opportunities for improvement include designing implants that reflect female anatomy, incorporating sex-based biological differences, and developing patient-specific replacements using modern imaging and 3D printing. Improvements should begin by understanding how male and female physiology affects hip replacement performance and longevity. Current implant geometries should be reanalyzed and redesigned to better suit female anatomy. Advances in imaging and 3D printing enable patient-specific implants rather than standardized devices. First, imaging systems can be trained on female CT scans to better capture anatomical differences. The system would then design custom implants, which researchers and physicians validate and adjust as needed, before 3D printing implants tailored to individual patients. Additionally, surgical protocols should be reevaluated to mitigate sex-based biases, while considering other demographic factors to create more inclusive standards for total hip arthroplasty. Considering diversity in engineering design improves safety, performance, and equity, ensuring that devices work for the full population.
